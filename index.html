Purpose of This Document

This document proposes a single structural change to the Denton Firemen's Relief and Retirement Fund (DFRRF) benefit formula: converting overtime from a Final Average Salary (FAS) component to a service credit at a 1.25:1 ratio. The document is intended to give the Board sufficient detail to direct Rudd and Wisdom, Inc. (R&W) to evaluate the proposal as a supplemental analysis in the December 31, 2025 actuarial valuation.

Two convictions drove this analysis:

1
We owe it to our members to give them maximum benefits paired with maximum long-term health of the fund. A generous benefit formula means nothing if the fund cannot sustain it. The goal is always both: the best benefit we can provide and a fund that can pay it for decades.
2
We owe it to our members to research viable alternatives that may result in increased health of our fund and increased member benefits. The alternative overtime proposal is exactly that: a structural change that the math shows would improve the fund's net overtime position by an estimated $10.5 million while still rewarding members for every hour of overtime they work.

Every assumption, input, and calculation is shown in full so R&W can identify any errors, validate against individual member data, and provide the Board with a definitive actuarial answer.

Executive Summary

The current benefit formula includes regular overtime in the Highest 36-Month Average Salary (FAS). This creates a structural mismatch: overtime worked before the highest 36 months generates contributions but no benefit, while overtime worked during the highest 36 months generates a lifetime benefit that far exceeds the contributions collected.

The December 31, 2023 actuarial valuation documented a $4,059,054 experience loss attributed primarily to greater-than-expected pay increases (Section II, page 8). That loss added 1.9 years to the amortization period.

The proposal: Remove overtime from the FAS calculation. Instead, credit each overtime hour with 1.25 hours of credited service. The benefit formula becomes:

Monthly Benefit = (Actual YOS + OT Service Credit) × 2.59% × Base FAS / 12

At the 1.25:1 ratio, every hour of overtime is net positive for the fund (+$2.19/hour). The fund goes from losing $7.8 million on overtime under the current system to retaining $2.8 million under the alternative, a total improvement of $10.5 million (71% of the fund's $14.82 million Unfunded Actuarial Accrued Liability, or UAAL).

What the Board is being asked to do: Direct Rudd and Wisdom to evaluate the overtime service credit conversion at 1.25:1, using individual member compensation and overtime data, as a supplemental analysis in the December 31, 2025 actuarial valuation. R&W's proposal calls for the Board to furnish all valuation data in April 2026. The data handoff is happening this month.

Part 1: The Structural Problem

A. What the Actuary Found

The December 31, 2023 actuarial valuation documented a $4,059,054 experience loss attributed "primarily" to greater-than-expected pay increases (Section II, page 8). That loss added 1.9 years to the amortization period (Section II, page 9).

The 2025 Review of Experience and Assumptions confirmed the pattern: actual covered payroll grew at 7.4% to 8.0% per year from 2014 to 2023, against a 3.0% assumption. R&W identified overtime variation as a contributor: "The amounts of overtime have varied from year to year" (Section G).

In response, the recommended compensation assumptions for the December 31, 2025 valuation add higher increases for service years 15 to 24 (4.55% and 5.58%, up from 3.0%). The actuary is pricing the overtime-driven pay deviation into future liabilities.

B. How the Current Formula Creates the Mismatch

The benefit formula (Plan Document Section B.2, Exhibit 14 Item 13) includes regular overtime in the Highest 36-Month Average Salary. The FAS is the average of the highest 36 consecutive months of total compensation, excluding lump sum sick leave and vacation payouts and special deployment overtime above $2,000 per biweekly pay period.

This creates two fundamentally different economic outcomes depending on when overtime is worked:

Before the highest 36 months ("pre-Max-3"): The fund collects contributions at 31.1% of overtime pay. No additional liability is created because the overtime does not affect the FAS. The member receives zero pension credit. Net to the fund: +$16.02 per hour.
During the highest 36 months ("Max 3"): The fund collects the same contributions at 31.1% of overtime pay. But the higher compensation flows through the entire benefit formula (Years of Service x 2.59% x FAS), generating a lifetime benefit increase. Net to the fund: -$398.76 per hour.

The fund loses nearly $400 on every hour of Max-3 overtime. A member working 1,200 overtime hours in a single year during their Max 3 costs the fund approximately $478,500 in that year alone. 1,200 hours represents the high end of what has been observed in recent payroll data; even 400 hours per year generates a $159,500 annual cost to the fund under the current formula.

Which Hours Earn OT Service Credit?

The City of Denton payroll system already classifies every hour worked as either a regular schedule pay code or an overtime pay code. The proposal rides that existing classification. No new definition is needed.

Earns OT Service Credit (1.25 hrs credited per hour worked)

All hours compensated under any overtime pay code in the City of Denton payroll system, including:

  • OT FLSA 1.5 — FLSA-mandated overtime, paid at 1.5x base hourly rate
  • OT CS 1.5 — City schedule overtime at time-and-a-half, including callbacks and voluntary OT
  • OT CS 1.0 — City schedule overtime at straight time
  • DisRel OT 1.5 — Disaster/deployment overtime at 1.5x
  • Any future overtime pay code the City creates

The credit is 1.25 hours of credited service per overtime hour worked, regardless of the rate at which the hour is compensated (1.0x, 1.5x, or any other overtime rate).

Does NOT Earn OT Service Credit (stays in normal YOS and Base FAS)

All hours compensated under regular schedule pay codes, including:

  • Regular Pay, Holiday, Vacation, Sick Leave
  • Educ Bach (Education Incentive), Paramed (Paramedic Pay), FF Adv (Advanced Firefighter)
  • Comm Svc Off (Community Service Officer), Long CS (Longevity)
  • Excess Life, and any other non-overtime compensation

These hours continue to accrue normal years of credited service and remain in the Base FAS calculation.

Why this works: the payroll system already enforces the classification, so there is no judgment call at retirement about which hours count. R&W can pull the Hrs/Units column from any overtime pay code in the payroll export and multiply by 1.25/2,912 to calculate the service credit. The classification is auditable from day one.

Part 3: Per-Hour Economics

A. Current System: Pre-Max-3 Hour

When a member works overtime outside their highest 36 months, the fund collects contributions but owes nothing additional:

Contributions collected: $51.51 x 31.1% = $16.02
Additional benefit liability: $0.00
Net to the fund: +$16.02 per hour

The member receives zero pension credit for this overtime. From the fund's perspective, pre-Max-3 overtime is pure revenue.

B. Current System: Max-3 Hour

When a member works overtime during their highest 36 months, the math reverses. The overtime pay flows into the FAS and permanently increases the lifetime benefit:

Step 1: FAS increase

One hour of overtime at $51.51/hour adds $51.51 to the member's annual compensation. If this overtime is worked consistently across all three years of the Max 3, the FAS increases by $51.51.

Step 2: Additional annual benefit

The benefit formula is Years of Service x 2.59% x FAS. The additional annual benefit from the FAS increase is:

25 × 0.0259 × $51.51 = $33.35 per year for life

Step 3: Present value of the lifetime benefit

At a 6.75% discount rate over 28 years (age 52 to 80), the present value annuity factor is:

(1 - 1.0675^(-28)) / 0.0675 = (1 - 0.16057) / 0.0675 = 12.4358

The present value of the lifetime benefit from one hour of Max-3 overtime is:

$33.35 × 12.4358 = $414.78

Step 4: Net cost to the fund

Contributions collected: $16.02. Benefit liability created: $414.78.

Net to the fund: $16.02 - $414.78 = -$398.76 per hour
For every dollar the fund collects in Max-3 overtime contributions, it owes $25.90 in future benefits.

C. Alternative System: Any Hour, Any Time

Under the proposed alternative, overtime is removed from the FAS. Instead, each overtime hour earns 1.25 hours of credited service, regardless of when in the member's career it is worked.

Step 1: Service credit per hour

1.25 / 2,912 = 0.000429 additional years of service

Step 2: Additional annual benefit

The benefit formula applies to the base FAS (no overtime). The additional benefit comes from the extra service credit:

0.000429 × 0.0259 × $100,000 = $1.11 per year for life

Step 3: Present value of the lifetime benefit

$1.11 × 12.4358 = $13.83

Step 4: Net to the fund

Contributions collected: $16.02 (unchanged). Benefit liability created: $13.83.

Net to the fund: $16.02 - $13.83 = +$2.19 per hour
Every hour of overtime is net positive for the fund under the alternative, regardless of when it is worked.

D. Breakeven Ratio

The 1.25:1 ratio was chosen because it is safely below the breakeven point. At a ratio R, the breakeven occurs when the PV of the benefit equals the contributions collected:

R × (0.0259 × $100,000 / 2,912) × 12.4358 = $16.02
R × 11.06 = $16.02
R = 1.449

Any ratio below 1.449:1 is net positive for the fund. The proposed 1.25:1 ratio provides a 14% margin of safety below the breakeven point. At 1.25:1, the fund retains $2.19 per hour. Even at 1.40:1, the fund would still break even, and members who work steady career overtime would receive 93% of their current overtime-related benefit.

E. Note for R&W: Straight-Time Overtime (OT CS 1.0)

The per-hour math above assumes all overtime is paid at 1.5x the base hourly rate. This is the case for the majority of OT hours (OT FLSA 1.5, OT CS 1.5, and DisRel OT 1.5). However, the City also has a straight-time overtime code (OT CS 1.0) paid at 1.0x the base hourly rate.

The service credit earned per hour is the same regardless of pay rate (1.25/2,912 = 0.000429 YOS). But contributions collected differ because 31.1% is assessed on the actual pay:

OT Pay RatePay/Hr ($100K base)Contribs CollectedPV Benefit CreatedNet to Fund
1.5x (FLSA, CS 1.5, DisRel)$51.51$16.02$13.83+$2.19
1.0x (OT CS 1.0)$34.34$10.68$13.83-$3.15

At 1.0x pay, the 1.25:1 ratio is slightly net negative (-$3.15/hour). The fund collects less in contributions but owes the same service credit. The breakeven ratio for straight-time OT is lower: 1.0x pay can only support a ratio of about 0.966:1 before turning net negative.

In practice, straight-time OT (OT CS 1.0) is a small fraction of total overtime hours. The vast majority of OT is paid at 1.5x. If, for example, 90% of OT hours are at 1.5x and 10% are at 1.0x, the blended net per hour is:

(0.90 × $2.19) + (0.10 × -$3.15) = $1.97 - $0.32 = +$1.66 per hour

The overall result remains net positive. However, R&W should model the actual mix of 1.0x vs. 1.5x hours from payroll data to confirm the blended result and determine whether the 1.25:1 ratio should apply uniformly to all OT codes or whether a lower ratio (such as 1.0:1) should apply to straight-time hours. The Board is open to either approach as long as the aggregate result is net beneficial to the fund.

Part 5: Why the Alternative Is Better for Members

A. The Current Plan Is a Gamble

The current plan is a gamble. It depends on two things going right at exactly the right time:

(a) Overtime has to be available during your Max 3. Staffing levels, budget cycles, and department needs change. A member who plans to work heavy overtime in their final three years may find that overtime simply is not there when they need it.

(b) Even if overtime is available, you have to be able to work enough of it to make a real difference in your FAS. Injury, illness, family obligations, or assignment changes can all limit how much overtime a member can realistically pick up during a specific 36-month window.

The alternative eliminates both of these risks. Every overtime hour earns the same service credit whether it is worked in year 1 or year 25. It does not matter whether the member works overtime during their lowest-paid years as a new firefighter or their highest-paid years as a senior officer. The credit is the same per hour. There is no timing strategy needed and no gamble about what overtime will be available in the final three years.

B. Early-Career Members Get Credit for the First Time

Under the current system, a firefighter who works 300 hours of mandatory overtime per year in their first 10 years receives zero pension credit for that work. The fund keeps the contributions and owes nothing. Under the alternative, every one of those hours earns 1.25 hours of credited service. For the first time, the heaviest overtime burden, which typically falls on the youngest, lowest-paid members, generates a retirement benefit.

C. Potential to Remove the Special Deployment Overtime Cap

Under the current plan, overtime pay for special deployments in excess of $2,000 per biweekly pay period is excluded from the FAS (Exhibit 14, Item 13). This cap exists because special deployment overtime can create large, sudden spikes in a member's FAS.

If the alternative is adopted and R&W confirm it is net beneficial to the fund, the Board could consider eliminating this cap entirely. Under the alternative, special deployment overtime would earn the same per-hour service credit as any other overtime, at a fixed and predictable cost to the fund. There would be no FAS spike to worry about, because overtime would no longer flow through the FAS at all.

D. Implementation: Member's Choice During Phase-In

For current employees: a 5-year phase-in window. During the phase-in, each member would choose which system they want to retire under. This is not a blend of both formulas. It is a choice. Members close to retirement can elect to stay under the current system. Members who prefer the service credit approach can opt in. At the end of the 5-year window, the alternative system applies to everyone.

For all future hires (effective date of adoption forward): immediate implementation under the new formula. New members would earn overtime service credit from day one, with no overtime in their FAS.

A change to the benefit formula for existing members requires a membership vote under the Texas Local Fire Fighters Retirement Act (TLFFRA).

Part 6: What We Are Asking Rudd and Wisdom to Do

Timing is critical. R&W's proposal for the 2026 to 2029 engagement (dated January 16, 2026) calls for the Board to furnish all valuation data in April 2026 and for R&W to complete the valuation by August 2026, with presentation to the Board in September 2026. The data handoff is happening this month. If the Board wants these analyses included, the request should accompany the data package the fund administrator sends to R&W.

Special studies are priced at hourly rates ($420/hour for the co-principal actuary per R&W's proposal).

Requested Analyses

(1) Validate or correct the per-hour economics shown in Part 3, using the full mortality table (PubS-2010 with generational projection), spousal continuation assumptions, and actual average salary data.

(2) Rerun the aggregate model in Part 4 using individual member compensation and overtime records from 2014 to 2025, replacing the estimated cohort distributions with actual data.

(3) Project the effect on the funded ratio and amortization period over 10 years under the alternative overtime treatment, modeled under the same six investment scenarios used in the December 31, 2023 valuation projections (Section II).

(4) Model the 5-year member-choice phase-in for current members and immediate implementation for new hires. Estimate the range of liability outcomes depending on the percentage of current members who elect each system.

(5) Identify any implementation issues, including interaction with the Retro DROP, the special deployment overtime cap (Exhibit 14, Item 13), and any TLFFRA statutory constraints.

Sequencing

Step 1: Board directs R&W to include the OT analysis in the December 31, 2025 valuation.

Step 2: R&W completes the analysis (target: August 2026).

Step 3: R&W presents findings to the Board (target: September 2026).

Step 4: If actuarial results confirm the projected improvement, Board presents the proposal to the membership for a vote.

Step 5: If adopted, implement (5-year choice window for current members, immediate for new hires).

Step 6: Allow one to two valuation cycles to confirm the improvement in fund metrics.

This analysis is for discussion purposes and does not constitute a formal actuarial valuation. The Board should engage Rudd and Wisdom, Inc. to perform actuarial analysis using individual member data before adopting any changes to the benefit formula.
